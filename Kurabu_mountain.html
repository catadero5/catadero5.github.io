Kurabu Mountain

Poem #39

梅花にほふ春べはくらぶ山やみにこゆれどしるくぞ有りける
ume no hana
niou harube wa
kurabuyama
yami ni koyuredo
shiruku zo arikeru
When springtime blossoms
glow on the flowering plum,
we know it at once,
though we travel without light
across the Hill of Darkness.

Poem #195

秋の夜の月のひかりしあかければくらぶの山もこえぬべらなり
aki no yo no
tsuki no hikari shi
akakereba
kurabu no yama mo
koenubera nari
Brilliant enough
to let a man find his way
even across dark-named
Kurabu Mountain: such is
moonlight on an autumn night.

Poem #295

わがきつる方もしられずくらぶ山木木のこのはのちるとまがふに
wa ga kitsuru
kata mo shirarezu
kurabuyama
kigi no ko no ha no
chiru to magau ni
Whence might I have come?
Every direction is one
on Kurabu Hill,
where the darkness is deepened
by countless scattering leaves.

Background Information

Similar to kurashi, meaning dark (McCullough). In all of these poems, this darkness is utilized.

Kurabu also means 'choose', and this also could be used for a pun. This is seen later in the Kokinshu in poem 590. There seems to be little to no scholarly information about Mount Kurabu; I have found one unreliable resource that claims that the ‘Waka Shogakusho' and 'Godaishu Utamakura’ place Kurabu Mountain at Yamashiro Province, so I have highlighted there.
